About Lisa

Professional Journey

I've been passionate about helping people all of my life. I started my career by studying exercise science and worked for many years in the fitness industry. As an exercise therapist, I specialised in helping clients with pain, injury and rehabilitation issues. In this work I became increasingly aware of the emotional toll of pain and injury and decided to study counselling to better help my clients.


This journey led to where I am today - with 13 years in private practice as a counsellor and psychotherapist who feels passionate about helping people find their way through difficult, uncertain or uncomfortable times. You could say that my journey started working with the body and has led me to working with the mind, although I do feel that the two are absolutely inseparable.

Approach & Philosophy

I believe in creating a safe, supportive, and encouraging space where you can explore your experiences without judgment. My approach is grounded in finding therapeutic wisdom - not just through theories or strategies, but through the lived experience of working through challenges together.


I enjoy working with all types of people and my clients come from many different ages, cultures and backgrounds. I see individuals, couples, and athletes - all at varying stages of life. This diversity is one of the things that makes counselling so rich and rewarding.

"The privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are." - C.G. Jung

Therapy Services

Almost everyone can benefit from counselling when they are feeling low, stressed, uncomfortable, unsure or in need of support. Counselling is sometimes called 'The Talking Cure' - talking about your concerns with someone who is objective, non-judgmental and supportive can help you feel better and think more clearly.

Individual Therapy

People come to counselling for many reasons - feeling down, anxious or overwhelmed, unsure about relationships, or navigating a new stage of life. Whatever the issue, exploring your experience with supportive guidance can help you gain clarity, feel more comfortable, become more confident and better understand yourself and others.

What to Expect

Sessions provide a safe space for you to explore your thoughts and feelings. We'll work together at a pace that feels right for you, building understanding and developing strategies for moving forward.

Couples Counselling

Couple counselling is about helping couples develop a deeper, more genuine and loving bond with one another. Couples can get stuck on issues or in patterns of relating that cause discomfort and unease. Through counselling, couples can grow to better understand and communicate with one another, resulting in a more loving, trusting and authentic relationship.

The Process

I provide a neutral, supportive environment where both partners can be heard and understood. Together we'll explore patterns, improve communication, and strengthen your connection.

Working with Athletes

Athletes face huge psychological and physical demands, often managing expectations from themselves and others that can leave them feeling exhausted and overwhelmed. The pressures of life can lead to depression and anxiety. It's normal for athletes to face identity, relationship, confidence and motivation challenges.

Specialized Support

I understand the unique pressures athletes face and provide tailored support for managing performance anxiety, transitions, injuries, and life beyond sport.

Clinical Supervision

For Therapists & Counsellors

I offer clinical supervision for therapists and counsellors at all stages of their professional journey. My supervision approach is informed by my PhD research on how therapists experience themselves appearing in their work, bringing a phenomenological lens to our explorations together.

What You'll Gain

  • Professional development and reflective practice
  • Support in finding your authentic therapeutic voice
  • Exploration of how you experience yourself in sessions
  • Working through challenges and disruptions
  • Growing therapeutic wisdom through experience

Format & Approach

Individual Supervision: One-on-one sessions tailored to your specific needs and practice context.

Group Supervision: Collaborative learning with fellow practitioners in a supportive environment.

Research & Expertise

PhD: The Phenomenon of the Psychotherapist's Self-Appearing

A Heideggerian Perspective

My doctoral research explores how experienced humanistically-informed psychotherapists experience their self-appearing during therapeutic practice, using Heideggerian hermeneutic phenomenology. By contrasting the phenomenon of the self-appearing with the dominant paradigm of self-awareness in psychotherapy, the study illuminates previously unexplored dimensions of therapists' lived experiences.

Key Research Findings

Through interviews with 12 experienced psychotherapists and a phenomenological discussion group with clinical supervisors, the research elucidates three key dimensions of the self-appearing process:

  • Being-in-Convention: Therapists' average everyday way of being, where pre-reflective conventions shape their practice
  • Being-in-Disruption: Experiencing challenges that disrupt habitual practices, initially leading to vulnerability and resistant attunement
  • Being-in-Transformation: Moving toward receptive attunement, holding vulnerability with openness, enabling deeper understanding and growth

Implications for Supervision

The research revealed four key themes for supervision practice:

  • Holding a phenomenological attitude in supervision
  • Allowing supervisees' disruptions to be present
  • Exploring disruptions experientially
  • Modelling working with disruption within the supervision space

Unique Contribution: This research shifts focus from therapists' "use of self" to understanding how therapists experience themselves appearing through a Heideggerian perspective. It demonstrates a pathway for growth in therapeutic wisdom that occurs not through theories or cognitive strategies but through the lived experience of working through disruption.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens in the first counselling session?
When you first come to counselling, you may feel a bit nervous or unsure. This is perfectly normal. The first session is all about getting to know each other. After a few practicalities (like filling in some paperwork and talking about fees and cancellation policies), I‚Äôll ask you to tell me a little bit about yourself and what brings you to counselling. We will talk through your concerns and goals and I will let you know if I feel that I can be of help. You are also free to ask any questions you might have. One of the most important predictors of a successful outcome from counselling is a good relationship between the client and counsellor. So I encourage you to trust yourself about this. Do you feel comfortable with me? Did you feel that I understood your concerns? If so, chances are that the work we do will be meaningful and helpful.
What about subsequent sessions?
In between counselling sessions it‚Äôs important to be curious and thoughtful about the things you are learning about yourself. It‚Äôs helpful to come to each session with something that you have been curious about or would like to work on. This gives direction to our work together and help to keep it centred around your goals.
How long does a counselling session last?
Counselling sessions last for one hour.
How often should I come?
There is no one answer to this. Often at the start of a counselling journey, it‚Äôs helpful to come weekly. This is especially important if the matter that brings you to counselling is pressing and you are feeling very distressed or overwhelmed. Many clients find weekly sessions the most helpful. Some clients choose to come fortnightly and that can also be fine. Sometimes clients come weekly at first and then switch to fortnightly when they are feeling more settled. All options are open and we will just keep talking about what is right for you.
How many sessions will I need?
Some people come to counselling with a very specific issue and feel better after only a few sessions. Others need more time, especially if they are noticing that similar issues arise over and over again in different contexts and relationships. This may be related to experiences from the past that take longer to work through. This is the difference between counselling (shorter term) and what is known as therapy (also called psychotherapy).
